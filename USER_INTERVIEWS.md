## Interview 1 — R.M., CTO, 8-person seed-stage SaaS startup

**Date:** 2026-05-05 | **Format:** 15-min Zoom

**Context:** Found via Indie Hackers Slack. Team of 8, all engineers, building a B2B analytics product.

**Direct quotes:**
- "We just pay whatever Cursor bills us. I honestly don't know what plan we're on — I think Business? Someone set it up."
- "I assumed Copilot and Cursor did different things. Do they overlap?"
- "If you told me I was wasting $400/month I'd feel stupid, but I'd also be grateful."

**Most surprising thing:** He had no idea his team had *both* Cursor Business and GitHub Copilot Enterprise active simultaneously — two coding assistants for the same devs. Combined: $59/seat/month for overlapping tools.

**What it changed:** Added an explicit "overlapping tools" warning to the audit results when a user inputs both Cursor and Copilot for the same coding use case. The audit now surfaces this as its own recommendation type.

## Interview 2 — P.K., Founder (solo), pre-revenue side project

**Date:** 2026-05-06 | **Format:** X DM → 10-min voice note exchange

**Context:** Replied to my tweet about AI tool costs. Solo founder, working nights/weekends.

**Direct quotes:**
- "I'm on Claude Pro AND ChatGPT Plus. They're basically the same for what I do."
- "I just keep paying because cancelling feels like I'm giving up on the project."
- "I've never actually compared them side by side, I just use whichever tab I have open."
- "Twenty dollars feels small. But I have four of these 'small' tools."

**Most surprising thing:** The psychological friction of cancelling — he framed it as "giving up" rather than optimizing. The audit tool needs to frame downgrade recommendations positively: "redirecting spend" not "cutting."

**What it changed:** Changed all recommendation copy to forward-looking framing ("Redirect $20/month toward tools that fit your actual workflow") rather than deficit framing ("You're wasting $20/month").

## Interview 3 — A.S., Engineering Manager, 22-person Series A startup

**Date:** 2026-05-07 | **Format:** 20-min call (referred by mutual contact)

**Context:** Manages 10 engineers. Company in fintech.

**Direct quotes:**
- "We had a board meeting where someone asked about AI tool ROI. I had no answer. That was embarrassing."
- "I can tell you our AWS bill to the dollar. I cannot tell you our AI tool bill without logging into five different admin panels."
- "What I actually want is something I can screenshot and put in a slide. That's the whole job."
- "Don't just give me a number, give me the reasoning. My CFO will ask."

**Most surprising thing:** The primary use case isn't saving money — it's *justifying* spend to non-technical stakeholders. The audit result is a communication tool as much as an optimization tool.

**What it changed:** Added the one-sentence reasoning to every recommendation card (not just the high-savings ones). Made the hero savings number screenshot-optimized. The "share link" button became prominent — the shareability is a feature, not an afterthought.
